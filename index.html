📋 FORM 1040 — Part 1: Filing Status (Top of Form)

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"The very first thing on the form is your 'Filing Status.' This just means: are you single? Married? Have kids you support alone? This matters because it changes how much tax you pay. Pick the ONE that fits you!"
Filing Status — Check One Box
☐ Single
You are NOT married. Most first-time filers check this box!
📌 Example: You're 22, have a job, not married → Check "Single"
Filing Status — Check One Box
☐ Married Filing Jointly (MFJ)
You AND your spouse file ONE return together. Usually saves the most money!
📌 Example: You married last year → Check this with your spouse
Filing Status — Check One Box
☐ Married Filing Separately (MFS)
You're married but file your own separate return. Usually NOT the best choice (you pay more). Only do this in special situations.
Filing Status — Check One Box
☐ Head of Household (HOH)
You're NOT married AND you pay more than half the cost of a home for yourself AND a child or dependent. Great deal — lower taxes than Single!
📌 Example: Single mom with a child living with her, pays the rent → HOH
Filing Status — Check One Box
☐ Qualifying Surviving Spouse (QSS)
Your spouse passed away in the last 2 years and you have a dependent child. You get the same low tax rate as Married Filing Jointly.
✅ First-time filer tip: Most first-time filers check SINGLE. If you're not sure, use e-file.com — it asks you questions to pick the right status automatically!

📋 FORM 1040 — Part 2: Your Personal Information

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"This part is like filling out a name tag at school! Just your name, address, and Social Security Number. Easy peasy! 🙂"
Line: First Name & Middle Initial / Last Name
Write your legal name exactly as it appears on your Social Security card. No nicknames!
Line: Social Security Number (SSN)
This is YOUR tax ID number. It's on your Social Security card. It looks like: 123-45-6789. Triple-check this — a wrong SSN is a major error!
📌 If you have a spouse, fill in their name and SSN below yours
Line: Home Address
Your current mailing address. This is where your refund check goes if you don't use direct deposit. Use your current address even if you moved this year.
Line: City, State, ZIP Code
Your full city, the 2-letter state abbreviation (like PA for Pennsylvania), and your 5-digit ZIP code.
Line: Presidential Election Campaign Fund
Do you want $3 of your taxes to go toward presidential campaign funding? This does NOT change your refund or what you owe. It's just a choice. Most people skip it.
Line: Digital Assets
Did you buy, sell, receive, or trade any cryptocurrency (like Bitcoin) or digital assets? If YES, check "Yes." If you have no idea what this is, check "No."
📌 Example: You bought Bitcoin on CoinBase this year → check Yes
Line: Standard Deduction Checkboxes
Check if: (a) someone else can claim you as a dependent, (b) your spouse is blind, or (c) you are 65 or older. These change your standard deduction amount.
Line: Dependents
A dependent is someone you take care of financially, like your child. For each dependent, you write their: name, SSN, relationship to you, and whether they qualify for the Child Tax Credit.
📌 Example: Your 8-year-old daughter → write her name, her SSN, "daughter," and check the child tax credit box
✅ Key Rule: To claim a child as a dependent, they must live with you more than half the year AND be under 19 (or under 24 if a full-time student).

💰 FORM 1040 — Part 3: Income (Lines 1–8)

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"Now we count ALL the money you made this year. From your job, from side gigs, from the bank — everything! Think of this like counting all the coins in your piggy bank. Every source goes here!"

Line 1 — Wages, Salaries, Tips

Line 1a — W-2 Box 1 Wages
Your main job pay MOST IMPORTANT
Look at your W-2 form from your employer. Find Box 1 — that's the number you put here. This is your total pay BEFORE taxes were taken out.
📌 Example: Your W-2 Box 1 says $32,500 → write $32,500 on Line 1a
Line 1b — Household Employee Wages Not on W-2
Did you pay a babysitter, nanny, or housekeeper $2,400 or more? The household worker puts their wages here. (Most first-time filers skip this.)
Line 1c — Tip Income Not Reported on W-2
If you're a server, bartender, delivery driver, or anyone who gets tips — and those tips were NOT included in your W-2 Box 1 — put the extra tips here.
📌 Example: You got $400 in cash tips your boss didn't report → write $400 here
Line 1d — Medicaid Waiver Payments
Special payments from Medicaid for caring for a disabled family member at home. Very specific — most filers skip this.
Line 1e — Taxable Dependent Care Benefits
If your employer helped pay for your child's daycare through a Flexible Spending Account, the amount over the limit goes here. Check your W-2 Box 10.
Line 1f — Employer Adoption Benefits
Money your employer gave you to help pay adoption costs that is above the excludable limit. Check W-2 Box 12 code T.
Line 1g — Wages from Form 8919
If you were incorrectly classified as an independent contractor when you should have been an employee, you use Form 8919 and put the wages here.
Line 1h — Other Earned Income
Any other wages not covered above. This includes taxable scholarships, prizes for work, or income from certain partnerships. Write the type on the dotted line.
Line 1z — Total Wages
ADD UP Lines 1a through 1h ADD THIS UP!
Add all the lines above together. This is your total wages and salary for the year.
📌 Example: Line 1a = $32,500, Line 1c = $400 → Line 1z = $32,900
✅ Tip: Have more than one W-2? (You worked two jobs?) Add BOTH Box 1 amounts together and put the total on Line 1a. Attach all W-2s!

Line 2 — Tax-Exempt Interest & Taxable Interest

Line 2a — Tax-Exempt Interest
Interest from municipal bonds or certain government savings bonds that you do NOT pay tax on. You still must report it, but it's not taxed. Find it on Form 1099-INT Box 8.
Line 2b — Taxable Interest PAY TAX ON THIS
Interest your bank or credit union paid you on savings accounts or CDs. Find it on Form 1099-INT Box 1. You pay taxes on this!
📌 Example: Your savings account paid you $45 in interest → write $45 on Line 2b. If over $1,500, also fill out Schedule B!

Line 3 — Dividends

Line 3a — Qualified Dividends
Dividends from stocks that qualify for a LOWER tax rate. Find it on Form 1099-DIV Box 1b. These are taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% — much lower than regular income!
Line 3b — Ordinary Dividends
ALL dividends you received. Find it on Form 1099-DIV Box 1a. This is the BIGGER number (it includes qualified dividends). If over $1,500, also fill Schedule B.

Line 4 — IRA Distributions

Line 4a — IRA Distributions (Total)
Total money you took OUT of your IRA (retirement savings account) this year. Find it on Form 1099-R Box 1.
Line 4b — Taxable Amount
The part of the IRA withdrawal you ACTUALLY pay tax on. For traditional IRA withdrawals after age 59½, this is usually the full amount. Find it on Form 1099-R Box 2a.
📌 Example: You took $5,000 out of your IRA. 1099-R shows $5,000 taxable → Line 4b = $5,000
⚠️ Warning: If you're under 59½ and took money out of your IRA, you usually pay a 10% penalty too! That goes on Schedule 2 Line 8. Professor Taxworth says: "Leave that retirement money alone until you're older! 🙂"

Line 5 — Pensions and Annuities

Line 5a — Total Pensions / Annuities
Total payments from pensions (retirement pay from old jobs) or annuities. From Form 1099-R Box 1.
Line 5b — Taxable Amount
The taxable portion. If your employer paid ALL contributions, the full amount is taxable. Find on Form 1099-R Box 2a.

Line 6 — Social Security Benefits

Line 6a — Total Social Security Benefits
Total Social Security payments received this year. Get this from Form SSA-1099 Box 5.
Line 6b — Taxable Social Security
The part of your Social Security that's taxable. This depends on your total income. If Social Security is your only income, it's usually $0 taxable! The IRS worksheet in the instructions tells you how to calculate this.
📌 Most retirees with low income: Line 6b = $0. Congrats!

Line 7 — Capital Gain or Loss

Line 7 — Capital Gain or (Loss)
Did you SELL stocks, real estate, or other investments? The profit or loss goes here. If you need to use Schedule D, that total flows here. If you only have simple transactions without Schedule D, you can sometimes enter directly.
📌 Example: You sold stock for $3,000 that you bought for $2,000 → $1,000 gain goes here (via Schedule D)

Line 8 — Other Income

Line 8 — Other Income (from Schedule 1, Line 10)
This is a catch-all for any other income that doesn't fit above. It comes from Schedule 1 Part I. Includes: gambling winnings, alimony received, rental income, business income, unemployment benefits, and more. We'll cover Schedule 1 below!

Line 9 — Total Income

Line 9 — TOTAL INCOME BIG TOTAL!
Add Lines 1z + 2b + 3b + 4b + 5b + 6b + 7 + 8
This is the BIG total of ALL your income for the year. Add up every income line. Don't skip any!
📌 Example: $32,900 wages + $45 interest + $0 dividends + $0 others = $32,945 total income

📉 FORM 1040 — Part 4: Adjustments to Income (Line 10)

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"Now we SUBTRACT some things from your total income! These are called 'adjustments' — they're like coupons that lower the amount you're taxed on. More adjustments = less tax. 🎉"
Line 10 — Adjustments to Income (from Schedule 1, Part II)
This number comes from Schedule 1 Part II, Line 26. Common adjustments include: student loan interest, IRA contributions, health insurance for self-employed people, and more. We'll go through all of them in the Schedule 1 section!

🔢 FORM 1040 — Part 5: Adjusted Gross Income (Line 11)

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"AGI is one of the most important numbers on your whole tax return! It's used to figure out your deductions and credits. Think of it like your 'official income' after taking some things off the top."
Line 11 — Adjusted Gross Income (AGI) KEY NUMBER!
Line 9 minus Line 10
Subtract your adjustments (Line 10) from your total income (Line 9). This is your AGI!
📌 Example: $32,945 income − $2,500 student loan interest = $30,445 AGI
💡 Why AGI matters: Your AGI determines: (1) how much of your deductions you can take, (2) whether you qualify for certain credits, and (3) your tax bracket. Keep this number handy!

📋 FORM 1040 — Part 6: Standard vs. Itemized Deductions (Lines 12–13)

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"Here's where you reduce your income MORE! Everyone gets a standard deduction automatically. It's like a free discount on your taxes. OR — if you have a LOT of deductible expenses — you can itemize (list them out) if that gives you a bigger deduction. Pick whichever is BIGGER!"

2024 Standard Deduction Amounts:

Filing StatusStandard Deduction
Single$14,600
Married Filing Jointly$29,200
Married Filing Separately$14,600
Head of Household$21,900
Age 65+ add-on (single)+$1,950
Age 65+ add-on (married)+$1,550 per spouse
Line 12 — Standard or Itemized Deduction
Take whichever is LARGER: your standard deduction amount (from table above) OR your Schedule A total (itemized). Most first-time filers use the standard deduction — it's already large enough!
📌 Example: You're single with $14,600 standard deduction vs. $9,000 in itemized → Take the standard $14,600!
Line 13 — Qualified Business Income Deduction (QBI)
If you have a small business or are self-employed, you may deduct up to 20% of your business income here. Use Form 8995 to calculate it. Skip if you're just a regular employee.
Line 14 — Total Deductions
Add Lines 12 + 13
Add your deduction (Line 12) and QBI deduction (Line 13) together.

💡 FORM 1040 — Part 7: Taxable Income (Line 15)

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"Almost there! Your taxable income is the number the IRS actually uses to calculate your tax. It's your AGI minus your deductions. Usually much lower than what you earned — thanks to those deductions!"
Line 15 — Taxable Income KEY NUMBER!
Line 11 (AGI) minus Line 14 (Deductions)
If this number is zero or below, you owe NO tax and may get money back! Never enter a negative number — enter $0.
📌 Example: $30,445 AGI − $14,600 standard deduction = $15,845 taxable income

💰 FORM 1040 — Part 8: Tax and Credits (Lines 16–24)

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"Now we calculate how much tax you owe on that taxable income. But THEN we subtract credits — and those are AWESOME because they reduce your tax dollar-for-dollar. Let's go!"
Line 16 — Tax
Look up your tax in the Tax Tables in the Instructions
Go to the IRS Tax Table (in the 1040 instructions booklet, or let software do it). Find your taxable income from Line 15 and your filing status. Read across to find your tax amount.
📌 Example: $15,845 taxable income, Single → look in the $15,800–$15,850 row → Tax = about $1,693
💡 Tax Brackets 2024 (Single): 10% on income up to $11,600 · 12% on $11,601–$47,150 · 22% on $47,151–$100,525. The bracket is on your LAST dollar earned, not your whole income!
Line 17 — Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT)
A special extra tax for higher-income people. Use Form 6251 to calculate. Most first-time filers: $0 here.
Line 18 — Add Lines 16 + 17
Just add Line 16 and Line 17 together. Usually just the same as Line 16 for most filers.
Line 19 — Child Tax Credit / Credit for Other Dependents
Up to $2,000 per child under 17! BIG CREDIT!
If you have children under age 17, you get up to $2,000 credit per child. Plus up to $500 for other dependents (like an elderly parent). This directly lowers your tax bill!
📌 Example: 2 kids under 17 → $4,000 credit! If your tax is $1,693, you owe $0 and may get more back via Additional Child Tax Credit!
Line 20 — Schedule 3, Line 8 Credits
Other credits from Schedule 3 go here. Includes education credits, retirement savings credit, child/dependent care credit, and more. See Schedule 3 section below!
Line 21 — Add Lines 19 + 20
Total of all your credits.
Line 22 — Subtract Line 21 from Line 18
Tax After Credits (cannot go below $0)
This is your tax AFTER credits. If Line 21 is bigger than Line 18, enter $0. Your credits wiped out your tax!
📌 Example: $1,693 tax − $2,000 child credit → Line 22 = $0
Line 23 — Other Taxes (Schedule 2, Part II)
Extra taxes from Schedule 2. Includes: self-employment tax, early IRA withdrawal penalty (10%), household employee taxes. We'll cover in Schedule 2!
Line 24 — Total Tax TOTAL TAX!
Line 22 + Line 23
This is your TOTAL tax liability for the year — everything you're supposed to pay. Now we compare this to what you ALREADY paid!

🏦 FORM 1040 — Part 9: Payments (Lines 25–32)

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"Here's the fun part — counting how much you ALREADY paid in taxes throughout the year! Most of it was taken out of your paycheck automatically. Let's add it all up!"
Line 25a — Federal Tax Withheld from W-2 MOST FILERS!
Look at your W-2 Box 2 — that's how much federal income tax your employer already sent to the IRS on your behalf all year. This is probably your biggest number here!
📌 Example: W-2 Box 2 = $3,200 → Line 25a = $3,200
Line 25b — Federal Tax Withheld from 1099
If any of your 1099 forms show federal tax was withheld (some do!), add those amounts here.
Line 25c — Other Withholding (Form 8959/8960)
Very rare — other withholding from certain special forms. Most filers: $0.
Line 26 — Estimated Tax Payments
Did you send the IRS money during the year on your own? (Self-employed people do this quarterly.) If yes, put the total amount you paid here. If you're a regular employee, this is usually $0.
Line 27 — Earned Income Credit (EIC/EITC) BIG REFUND BOOSTER!
Up to $7,830 — ONE OF THE BEST CREDITS!
This credit is for people who WORK but don't earn a lot. Even if you owe $0 in tax, you can still GET this money back as a refund! The amount depends on your income and how many children you have. Use the EITC worksheet in the instructions to calculate.
📌 3 kids + income around $30,000 → could get up to $7,830 back! 🎉
Line 28 — Additional Child Tax Credit (Schedule 8812)
If your Child Tax Credit (Line 19) was MORE than your tax, the leftover can come back to you as a refund — up to $1,700 per child! This is the "refundable" part.
Line 29 — American Opportunity Credit (Form 8863)
Up to $1,000 refundable for college education expenses. Even if you owe $0, you can get 40% of this credit back as cash! We'll cover Form 8863 below.
Line 30 — Reserved / Future Use
Currently blank — skip this line.
Line 31 — Amount from Schedule 3, Line 15
Refundable credits from Schedule 3 (like Premium Tax Credit for health insurance purchased through the marketplace).
Line 32 — Other Payments and Refundable Credits
From Form 2439, Form 4136, or Form 8885. Very rare for first-time filers.
Line 33 — Total Payments ADD THEM UP!
Add Lines 25d + 26 + 27 + 28 + 29 + 31 + 32
This is everything you've paid in taxes + refundable credits. Compare to Line 24 to see if you get money back!

🎉 FORM 1040 — Part 10: Refund or Amount You Owe (Lines 34–38)

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"THE MOMENT OF TRUTH! 🎉 Compare what you owe (Line 24) vs. what you already paid (Line 33). If you paid more → you get a refund! If you paid less → you owe a little more. Either way, you've figured it out!"
Line 34 — IF Line 33 > Line 24: OVERPAID → REFUND! REFUND! 🎉
Subtract Line 24 from Line 33. The difference is your refund! Enter it here.
📌 Example: Paid $3,200 − Owed $0 tax = $3,200 refund! 🎉
Line 35a — Refund Amount / Direct Deposit
How much of your refund do you want deposited directly to your bank? (Most people: all of it!) Fastest way to get your money — as few as 8 days if you e-file!
Lines 35b, 35c, 35d — Bank Routing and Account Number
35b = Bank routing number (9 digits at bottom left of check). 35c = Checking or Savings. 35d = Account number. This is how the IRS sends your refund directly to your bank!
Line 36 — Amount Applied to Next Year
Do you want some of your refund kept on account for NEXT year's taxes? Most people: $0 — take the whole refund now!
Line 37 — Amount You OWE PAY THIS!
IF Line 24 > Line 33: subtract Line 33 from Line 24. This is how much more you need to pay. Pay by April 15!
📌 Example: Owe $500, paid $400 → Line 37 = $100 due
Line 38 — Estimated Tax Penalty
If you're self-employed and didn't pay enough quarterly estimated taxes, there's a small penalty here. Use Form 2210. Most regular employees: $0.
✅ How to Pay What You Owe: Go to IRS.gov/pay or use Direct Pay. Or mail a check payable to "United States Treasury" with your SSN and "2024 Form 1040" in the memo.

✍️ FORM 1040 — Part 11: Sign and Date Your Return

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"Almost done! An unsigned tax return is INVALID — like a check without a signature. Don't forget this step! By signing, you're saying everything is true to the best of your knowledge."
Signature
Sign your name. If married filing jointly, BOTH spouses must sign. The form says 'Under penalty of perjury...' — this means you're swearing the information is correct.
Date
Write today's date. The deadline is April 15!
Your Occupation
Write your job title. Examples: "Server," "Student," "Warehouse Worker," "Software Developer." Just be honest!
Identity Protection PIN (IP PIN)
If the IRS sent you a 6-digit IP PIN (because your SSN was stolen or you signed up for one), enter it here. If you don't have one, leave blank.
Paid Preparer Section
ONLY filled in if you paid a tax professional (like H&R Block or a CPA) to prepare your return. If you did it yourself, leave blank.
Third Party Designee
Do you want to let someone else (a friend, family member) talk to the IRS about your return if they have questions? Check "Yes" and fill in their name and phone number. Otherwise, check "No."

📄 SCHEDULE 1 — Additional Income & Adjustments

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"Schedule 1 has two parts. Part I: more types of income (like business income, unemployment, gambling). Part II: adjustments that reduce your income (like student loan interest). Only fill this out if any of these apply to you!"

Schedule 1 — Part I: Additional Income

Schedule 1, Line 1 — Taxable Refunds of State/Local Taxes
Did you get a state tax refund last year AND you itemized deductions? That refund might be taxable. Check the worksheet in the instructions. If you took the standard deduction last year, skip this — it's $0.
Schedule 1, Line 2a & 2b — Alimony Received
Money received as alimony from a divorce BEFORE January 1, 2019 is taxable income. Post-2018 divorces: not taxable. Enter the amount and your ex-spouse's SSN.
Schedule 1, Line 3 — Business Income (Schedule C)
If you have a small business, are self-employed, or do freelance work (gig apps like Uber, DoorDash, Etsy), your net profit from Schedule C goes here.
Schedule 1, Line 4 — Other Gains (Form 4797)
Profit from selling business property (like a work truck or equipment). Skip if you didn't sell business assets.
Schedule 1, Line 5 — Rental Real Estate, Royalties, Partnerships (Schedule E)
Income or loss from rental properties, book royalties, partnerships, or S-corps goes here via Schedule E.
Schedule 1, Line 6 — Farm Income (Schedule F)
If you own or operate a farm, net income goes here via Schedule F.
Schedule 1, Line 7 — Unemployment Compensation
UNEMPLOYMENT IS TAXABLE! IMPORTANT!
Did you receive unemployment benefits? The FULL AMOUNT is taxable. Find it on Form 1099-G Box 1. Many people forget this and get a surprise bill!
📌 Example: Got $8,500 in unemployment → Line 7 = $8,500. You'll owe some tax on this!
Schedule 1, Line 8 — Other Income (Many Sub-lines)
Catch-all for many income types:
8a — Net gambling/lottery winnings (from W-2G)
8b — Cancellation of debt (1099-C) — when a bank forgives a debt, it's income!
8c — Foreign income exclusion
8d — Taxable health savings account distributions
8e — Alaska Permanent Fund dividends
8f — Wages earned while in prison
8g — Reforestation expenses
8h — Recapture of charitable deductions
8i — Income from Form 8853/8889/8853 (HSA/Archer MSA)
8j — Medicaid waiver payments included in income
8k — Pension distributions from nonqualified plans
8l — Distributions from ABLE accounts
8m — Recapture of first-time homebuyer credit
8n — Income from Form 8278
8o — Income previously deferred under nonqualified plans
8p — Section 951 inclusions
8z — Other income (write in the type)
Schedule 1, Line 10 — Total Additional Income
Add Lines 1–8. This goes to Form 1040 Line 8.

Schedule 1 — Part II: Adjustments to Income (Deductions Above the Line)

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"These adjustments are GREAT because you can take them even if you use the standard deduction! They directly lower your income before anything else."
Schedule 1, Line 11 — Educator Expenses
Are you a teacher, teacher's aide, counselor, or principal (K–12)? You can deduct up to $300 of your own money spent on classroom supplies — without needing receipts over $300 (but keep them anyway!).
Schedule 1, Line 12 — Certain Business Expenses (Reservists, Artists, Gov Officials)
Specific deductions for military reservists traveling over 100 miles, qualifying performing artists, and fee-based government officials. Very specific — most skip this.
Schedule 1, Line 13 — Health Savings Account (HSA) Deduction (Form 8889)
Did you contribute to an HSA (Health Savings Account) with your own money — NOT through your employer's payroll? Those contributions are deductible! Use Form 8889 Part I.
Schedule 1, Line 15 — Student Loan Interest Deduction
Up to $2,500! POPULAR!
Paid interest on student loans? You can deduct up to $2,500! Phases out at higher incomes. Check Form 1098-E for the amount. You DON'T have to itemize to get this!
📌 Example: Paid $1,800 interest on student loans → Line 15 = $1,800 (saves you about $200+ in taxes!)
Schedule 1, Line 16 — Tuition and Fees (If active — check current year)
Tuition deduction — check if this expired for the current year (it sometimes does). Education credits on Form 8863 are often better anyway.
Schedule 1, Line 17 — Penalty on Early Withdrawal of Savings
If your bank charged you a penalty for withdrawing a CD (certificate of deposit) early, that penalty is deductible! Find it on Form 1099-INT Box 2.
Schedule 1, Line 19 — Self-Employed Health Insurance Deduction
If you're self-employed and paid your own health, dental, or vision insurance premiums, you can deduct 100% of those premiums! Big deduction for freelancers.
Schedule 1, Line 20 — Self-Employed SEP, SIMPLE, Qualified Plans
Did you contribute to a SEP-IRA or SIMPLE IRA as a self-employed person? That contribution is deductible — can be up to 25% of net self-employment income!
Schedule 1, Line 21 — IRA Deduction
Contributed to a Traditional IRA? Depending on your income and whether you have a workplace retirement plan, up to $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+) may be deductible.
Schedule 1, Line 22 — Student Loan Interest (Already covered Line 15)
Schedule 1, Line 24 — Other Adjustments
Includes: jury duty pay given to employer, attorney fees for discrimination cases, contributions to Archer MSA, reforestation amortization, and repayment of supplemental unemployment benefits.
Schedule 1, Line 26 — Total Adjustments
Add all adjustments together. This goes to Form 1040 Line 10.

📄 SCHEDULE 2 — Additional Taxes

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"Schedule 2 lists extra taxes that some people have to pay. If none of these apply to you — great, you skip it! Most simple filers won't need this."

Schedule 2 — Part I: Alternative Minimum Tax

Schedule 2, Line 1 — Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT)
A parallel tax system to make sure higher-income people pay a minimum amount. Calculated on Form 6251. Most first-time filers: $0. AMT exemption for 2024: $85,700 (single), $133,300 (married).
Schedule 2, Line 2 — Excess Advance Premium Tax Credit Repayment
If you received too much Advanced Premium Tax Credit (health insurance subsidy through Healthcare.gov) based on estimated income, you must pay some back here.

Schedule 2 — Part II: Other Taxes

Schedule 2, Line 4 — Self-Employment Tax (from Schedule SE)
If you're self-employed, you pay 15.3% for Social Security and Medicare on your business income. This goes here from Schedule SE. (But you get to deduct half of it on Schedule 1!)
Schedule 2, Line 6 — Social Security and Medicare Tax on Unreported Tips
If you received cash tips and didn't report them to your employer, you pay the SS/Medicare tax here.
Schedule 2, Line 7a — Uncollected Social Security/Medicare Tax from W-2
Rare — if a W-2 shows uncollected Social Security or Medicare tax in Box 12 with codes A or B.
Schedule 2, Line 8 — Additional Tax on IRAs, Retirement Plans (Form 5329)
The 10% early withdrawal penalty if you took money from a retirement account before age 59½. Also covers required minimum distribution failures (50% penalty!) and excess IRA contributions.
📌 Example: Took $5,000 early from IRA → 10% penalty = $500 on Line 8
Schedule 2, Line 11 — Household Employment Taxes (Schedule H)
If you paid a nanny, housekeeper, or other household employee $2,400 or more, you owe their employer share of SS/Medicare taxes. Use Schedule H.
Schedule 2, Line 12 — First-Year RMD Failure
If you turned 73 and failed to take Required Minimum Distributions from your IRA or 401(k), there's an excise tax. Use Form 5329.
Schedule 2, Line 17 — Other Additional Taxes
Net Investment Income Tax (3.8% on investment income for higher earners, Form 8960), Additional Medicare Tax (0.9% on wages over $200K, Form 8959), and others.

📄 SCHEDULE 3 — Additional Credits & Payments

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"Schedule 3 is GOOD news — it's more credits and payments that reduce what you owe! These are things that couldn't fit on the main form. Check each one!"

Schedule 3 — Part I: Nonrefundable Credits

Schedule 3, Line 1 — Foreign Tax Credit (Form 1116)
Did you pay taxes to a foreign country on income earned abroad? You get a dollar-for-dollar credit for those taxes so you're not taxed twice. Use Form 1116.
Schedule 3, Line 2 — Child and Dependent Care Credit (Form 2441)
Up to $1,050 for 1 child, $2,100 for 2+ POPULAR!
Paid for daycare, preschool, or aftercare so you could work or look for work? You get a credit for that! Use Form 2441. Qualifying expenses: up to $3,000 per child (max 2 children).
📌 Example: Paid $5,000 in daycare for 2 kids, earned $40,000 → Credit = about $600
Schedule 3, Line 3 — Education Credits (Form 8863)
American Opportunity Credit: up to $2,500! BIG!
Two credits for college:
American Opportunity Credit (AOC): Up to $2,500 per year for first 4 years of college. Must be enrolled at least half-time. 40% is refundable ($1,000).
Lifetime Learning Credit (LLC): Up to $2,000. Available for any year of college or grad school. NOT refundable.
Schedule 3, Line 4 — Retirement Savings Contributions Credit (Form 8880)
The "Saver's Credit" — up to $1,000! OFTEN MISSED!
Did you contribute to a 401(k), IRA, or other retirement account? If your income is below about $36,500 (single) or $73,000 (married), you get a credit just for saving for retirement! Up to $1,000 credit.
Schedule 3, Line 5a — Residential Clean Energy Credit (Form 5695)
Installed solar panels, wind energy, geothermal heat pump, or battery storage at your home? Get 30% of the cost as a credit! No cap on this one.
Schedule 3, Line 5b — Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit
Put in energy-efficient windows, doors, insulation, heat pumps, water heaters? Up to $3,200 per year in credits (from Form 5695).
Schedule 3, Line 6 — Other Nonrefundable Credits
Includes: General Business Credit (Form 3800), Credit for Prior Year Minimum Tax (Form 8801), Adoption Credit (Form 8839), Mortgage Interest Credit (Form 8396), and several others.

Schedule 3 — Part II: Other Payments and Refundable Credits

Schedule 3, Line 9 — Net Premium Tax Credit (Form 8962)
Health Insurance Marketplace Credit HEALTHCARE!
If you bought health insurance through Healthcare.gov (the Marketplace) and qualify based on income, you get a credit to help pay premiums. Calculate on Form 8962.
Schedule 3, Line 10 — Amount on Extension (Form 4868)
If you filed for a tax extension and paid some tax at that time, enter that payment here so you get credit for it.
Schedule 3, Line 11 — Excess Social Security Tax Withheld
Worked two jobs? If both employers withheld Social Security tax and together they took out more than the max ($10,453.20 for 2024), you get the excess back as a credit!
Schedule 3, Line 15 — Total Credits and Payments
Total of all Schedule 3 items. Goes to Form 1040 Lines 20 and 31.

📋 SCHEDULE A — Itemized Deductions

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"Most people take the standard deduction (easier!). But if you have a LOT of deductible expenses — like a mortgage, high medical bills, or big charitable donations — itemizing might save you more! Compare both and take the BIGGER one."
Schedule A, Lines 1–4 — Medical and Dental Expenses
You can deduct medical expenses that exceed 7.5% of your AGI. So if your AGI is $40,000 × 7.5% = $3,000 threshold. If you spent $5,000 on medical, you deduct $2,000.
📌 What counts: doctor visits, surgeries, prescriptions, glasses, dental, hearing aids, therapy, insurance premiums you pay yourself
Schedule A, Lines 5–6 — State and Local Taxes (SALT)
Capped at $10,000! CAP!
You can deduct state income taxes OR sales taxes (pick one), PLUS property taxes. But the total SALT deduction is capped at $10,000 ($5,000 if married filing separately).
Schedule A, Lines 8–9 — Mortgage Interest
Interest paid on your home mortgage (from Form 1098). For loans taken after Dec 16, 2017, deductible on the first $750,000 of loan. This is often the biggest itemized deduction!
Schedule A, Line 10 — Mortgage Insurance Premiums
PMI (private mortgage insurance) you paid may be deductible. Check if this provision is currently active for your tax year.
Schedule A, Lines 11–14 — Charitable Contributions
Money or goods donated to qualified charities (churches, Red Cross, food banks, etc.). Cash donations need a receipt or canceled check. Goods (like clothing to Goodwill) need a receipt showing fair market value.
📌 Example: Donated $1,200 to your church + $400 in clothes to Goodwill = $1,600 charitable deduction
Schedule A, Lines 15–16 — Casualty & Theft Losses
Only deductible for losses in a federally declared disaster area (hurricane, tornado, wildfire, flood). Regular theft or accidents are NOT deductible since 2018.
Schedule A, Line 16 — Other Itemized Deductions
Gambling losses (up to gambling winnings only), casualty/theft losses from disaster areas, amortizable bond premium, federal estate tax on income in respect of a decedent.
Schedule A, Line 17 — Total Itemized Deductions
Add everything together. Compare to your standard deduction — use whichever is LARGER on Form 1040 Line 12!

📋 SCHEDULE B — Interest and Ordinary Dividends

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"You only need Schedule B if your bank interest is over $1,500 OR your dividends are over $1,500. If below those amounts, just put the totals on Form 1040 directly — no Schedule B needed!"
Schedule B, Part I — Interest
List each bank/institution that paid you interest, with the amount. Add them all up. Total goes to Form 1040 Line 2b.
Schedule B, Part II — Dividends
List each company or fund that paid you dividends, with the amount. Total ordinary dividends go to Form 1040 Line 3b.
Schedule B, Part III — Foreign Accounts and Trusts
Answer yes/no: Did you have a financial account in a foreign country? Do you have authority over a foreign trust? These are required disclosure questions.

💼 SCHEDULE C — Profit or Loss from Business (Self-Employed)

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"Do you drive for Uber, sell on Etsy, do freelance work, mow lawns for money, or run any kind of business? Schedule C is for YOU! You report all your income AND subtract your business expenses. Only pay tax on the profit!"
Schedule C, Part I — Income
Line 1: Gross receipts or sales — ALL money your business received
Line 2: Returns and allowances — refunds you gave customers
Line 3: Subtract Line 2 from Line 1
Line 4: Cost of goods sold (from Part III)
Line 5: Gross profit = Line 3 minus Line 4
Line 6: Other income (finance charges, prizes, etc.)
Line 7: Total gross income (Line 5 + Line 6)
Schedule C, Part II — Expenses SAVE MONEY HERE!
Line 8: Advertising (flyers, Google ads, Facebook ads)
Line 9: Car and truck expenses (Form 4562 or standard mileage: 67¢/mile in 2024)
Line 10: Commissions and fees paid to others
Line 11: Contract labor (paid other contractors)
Line 12: Depletion (for natural resource businesses)
Line 13: Depreciation of assets (Form 4562)
Line 14: Employee benefit programs
Line 15: Insurance (business)
Line 16: Interest on business loans
Line 17: Legal and professional fees (accountant, lawyer)
Line 18: Office expense (pens, paper, printer ink)
Line 19: Pension and profit-sharing plans
Line 20: Rent or lease (equipment, office space)
Line 21: Repairs and maintenance
Line 22: Supplies used in business
Line 23: Taxes and licenses (business licenses, state taxes)
Line 24: Travel expenses (airfare, hotels for business)
Line 24b: Deductible meals (50% of actual business meals)
Line 25: Utilities (if separate business location)
Line 26: Wages paid to employees
Line 27: Other expenses (list them all on Part V)
Schedule C, Line 28 — Total Expenses
Add all expense lines together.
Schedule C, Line 30 — Home Office Deduction (Form 8829)
Do you have a room in your home used ONLY for business? You may deduct a percentage of your rent/mortgage, utilities, and insurance. Simplified method: $5 per square foot up to 300 sq ft = max $1,500.
Schedule C, Line 31 — Net Profit or (Loss) KEY NUMBER!
Line 7 minus Line 28 minus Line 30
Your business profit. This goes to Schedule 1 Line 3 and is ALSO subject to self-employment tax on Schedule SE!
📌 Example: Made $20,000 driving Uber, spent $8,000 on gas/miles/phone → Net profit = $12,000
⚠️ Self-Employed Warning: Your Schedule C profit is taxed TWICE — once as income tax AND once as self-employment tax (15.3%). Plan ahead and set aside about 25-30% of profits for taxes!

📈 SCHEDULE D — Capital Gains and Losses

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"Sold stocks, cryptocurrency, real estate, or other investments? Schedule D is where you report the profit or loss. Good news: if you held the investment for over a year, the tax rate is LOWER than regular income!"
Short-Term vs. Long-Term — THE KEY DIFFERENCE
Short-term: Held 1 year or LESS → taxed at your regular income tax rate (could be 22% or more)
Long-term: Held MORE than 1 year → taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% — much lower!
📌 Example: Bought stock Jan 1, sold Dec 31 = short-term. Bought Jan 1, sold Jan 2 next year = long-term!
Schedule D, Part I — Short-Term Capital Gains and Losses (Lines 1–7)
Line 1a: Transactions with basis reported to IRS (from 1099-B, Box A)
Line 1b: Transactions with basis NOT reported to IRS (Box B)
Line 2: Short-term transactions from other forms (Form 6252, 4684, etc.)
Line 3: Short-term from partnerships/S-corps (Schedule K-1)
Line 4: Short-term capital loss carryover from prior year
Line 7: Net short-term capital gain or loss (add Lines 1–6)
Schedule D, Part II — Long-Term Capital Gains and Losses (Lines 8–15)
Line 8a: Long-term transactions with basis reported to IRS
Line 8b: Long-term transactions without basis reported
Line 9: Long-term from other forms
Line 10: Long-term from partnerships/S-corps (K-1)
Line 11: Long-term gain from Form 4797
Line 12: Capital gain distributions
Line 13: Long-term capital loss carryover from prior year
Line 15: Net long-term capital gain or loss
Schedule D, Part III — Summary (Lines 16–22)
Line 16: Combine Line 7 and Line 15 — total net capital gain or loss
Line 17: Is Line 16 a gain? Use Qualified Dividends and Capital Gain Tax Worksheet
Line 21: If LOSS — you can deduct up to $3,000 against ordinary income this year
Line 22: Excess losses carry over to next year (capital loss carryover)
💡 Silver lining on losses: If you lost money on investments, you can use up to $3,000 of capital losses to reduce other income each year. Unused losses carry forward to future years forever!

🏠 SCHEDULE E — Rental Income, Royalties, Partnerships

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"Do you rent out a property, receive book royalties, or own part of a partnership or S-corp? Schedule E is your form! You report income AND expenses — so deductions are key here."

Schedule E — Part I: Rental and Royalty Income

Schedule E, Lines 1–2 — Property Information
Address of the rental property. Type of property: single-family, multi-family, vacation/short-term rental, commercial, land, royalties, etc. Fair rental days and personal use days.
Schedule E, Lines 3 — Rents Received
Total rent you received from tenants all year. This is ALL payments — not just checks, also Venmo, Zelle, cash, etc.
Schedule E, Lines 4 — Royalties Received
Royalties from books, music, patents, oil and gas rights, etc. From Form 1099-MISC Box 2.
Schedule E, Lines 5–18 — Rental Expenses
Line 5: Advertising (Zillow, Craigslist, etc.)
Line 6: Auto/travel for rental management
Line 7: Cleaning and maintenance
Line 8: Commissions (property manager fees)
Line 9: Insurance
Line 10: Legal and other professional fees
Line 11: Management fees
Line 12: Mortgage interest (from Form 1098)
Line 13: Other interest
Line 14: Repairs
Line 15: Supplies
Line 16: Taxes (property taxes)
Line 17: Utilities (if you pay them)
Line 18: Depreciation (Form 4562 — BIG deduction! Properties depreciate over 27.5 years)
Schedule E, Lines 20–26 — Income or Loss Summary
Total income minus expenses = profit or loss per property. If you're a non-passive investor (active participation), losses may be deductible up to $25,000 if AGI is below $100,000.

Schedule E — Part II: Partnerships and S-Corps

Schedule E, Lines 27–43 — Partnership/S-Corp Income
If you own part of a partnership or S-corporation, you receive a Schedule K-1 from them. Transfer the ordinary income/loss from K-1 Box 1 here. Also shows: interest, dividends, capital gains, charitable contributions, and other items from the business.

Schedule E — Part III–IV: Estates, Trusts, REMICs, Other

Schedule E, Lines 33–43
Income from being a beneficiary of an estate or trust (Schedule K-1 from the trust/estate). Also for Real Estate Mortgage Investment Conduits (REMIC) income. Very specific situations.

🧾 SCHEDULE SE — Self-Employment Tax

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"When you work for a company, they pay HALF your Social Security and Medicare taxes for you. When you work for yourself, YOU pay BOTH halves — 15.3%! That's the self-employment tax. BUT you get to deduct half of it back on your return. Fair trade!"
Schedule SE, Line 2 — Net Profit from Schedule C / F
Your business net profit from Schedule C (or farm profit from Schedule F). This is the starting point for SE tax. If you have a LOSS, you usually owe $0 SE tax.
Schedule SE, Line 3 — Multiply by 92.35%
Why 92.35%? Because employees get to deduct the employer half (7.65%) before SE tax is calculated. So you multiply your profit by 0.9235 to be fair.
📌 Example: $12,000 profit × 0.9235 = $11,082 subject to SE tax
Schedule SE, Line 4 — Multiply by 15.3%
15.3% = 12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare. Applied to income up to $168,600 (2024 Social Security wage base). Income above that only owes 2.9% Medicare.
📌 Example: $11,082 × 15.3% = $1,696 SE tax
Schedule SE, Line 12 — SE Tax Due
This amount goes to Schedule 2 Line 4, and then to Form 1040 Line 23.
Deduction: Half of SE Tax
You get to deduct HALF of your SE tax as an adjustment to income (Schedule 1 Line 15). The IRS gives you this deduction because employees don't pay the employer half. So from the example above: $1,696 ÷ 2 = $848 deduction!
💡 SE Tax Rule of Thumb: Set aside about 25-30% of every dollar your business makes for taxes (income tax + SE tax). Pay quarterly estimated taxes to avoid an underpayment penalty.

📎 Other Key Forms for 1040

🧑‍🏫
Professor Taxworth says:
"There are a few other forms that often come up for regular filers! You only fill out the ones that apply to your situation."

Form 8863 — Education Credits

Part I — American Opportunity Credit (Lines 1–15)
For first 4 years of college. 100% of first $2,000 + 25% of next $2,000 = max $2,500. Must be at least half-time enrolled. $1,000 (40%) is refundable even if you owe no tax! Qualified expenses: tuition, fees, books, supplies required for enrollment.
Part II — Lifetime Learning Credit (Lines 10–19)
20% of first $10,000 in tuition = max $2,000. Available for any years of college, grad school, or job skills courses. NOT refundable (only reduces tax to $0).

Form 2441 — Child and Dependent Care Expenses

Key Lines on Form 2441
Part I: Name, address, and EIN/SSN of your daycare provider (required!)
Part II, Line 3: Names and SSNs of children/dependents who received care
Part II, Line 6: Qualifying expenses per child (up to $3,000 for 1, $6,000 for 2+)
Part II, Line 8: Lower of your earned income, spouse's income, or qualifying expenses
Part II, Line 10: Credit percentage (20-35% based on income)
Part II, Line 11: Credit amount (goes to Schedule 3 Line 2)

Form 8829 — Home Office Expenses

Key Lines on Form 8829
Lines 1–3: Area of home office ÷ Total home area = business use percentage
Lines 9–21: Actual expenses × business % = deductible amount
Expenses: mortgage interest, rent, utilities, insurance, repairs, depreciation
Alternative simplified method: $5 × square footage (max 300 sq ft = $1,500 max)

Form 8889 — Health Savings Account (HSA)

Key Lines on Form 8889
Part I (Contributions): 2024 contribution limits: $4,150 individual, $8,300 family. Contributions deductible if made with your own money.
Part II (Distributions): Report HSA withdrawals. Medical expenses = tax-free! Non-medical = taxable + 20% penalty (if under 65).

Form 5329 — Retirement Plan Additional Taxes

Key Lines on Form 5329
Part I: Early distributions (before 59½) — 10% penalty. Many exceptions apply (disability, medical expenses, first home purchase, education, etc.)
Part VIII: Excess IRA contributions — 6% penalty per year until corrected
Part IX: Missed required minimum distributions — 25% penalty (reduced to 10% if corrected quickly)

Form 8962 — Premium Tax Credit (Health Insurance Marketplace)

Key Lines on Form 8962
Lines 1–5: Family size and household income as % of federal poverty level
Lines 12–23: Monthly premium assistance (credit) vs. advance payments made
Line 24: If credit > advance payments → get the difference back as a refund
Line 29: If advance payments > credit → repay the excess (capped at certain amounts)

Form 4868 — Automatic Extension of Time to File

Form 4868 — Getting More Time to File
File by April 15 to get 6 more months to FILE (until October 15). But this is NOT more time to PAY! You must still estimate and pay any tax owed by April 15 or face interest and penalties.
How to file: Super easy — just submit Form 4868 electronically by April 15. No explanation needed. Automatically granted!
